# Valuation Methods Reference

## Price-Based Ratios

### Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
- **Formula**: Stock Price / Earnings Per Share
- **Interpretation**:
  - Compare to industry average P/E
  - Compare to company's historical P/E range
  - Lower P/E may indicate undervaluation (or justified concerns)
  - Higher P/E may indicate growth expectations (or overvaluation)
- **Limitations**: Not useful for unprofitable companies
- **Industry Context**:
  - Tech/Growth: P/E 20-40+ common
  - Mature/Value: P/E 10-20 typical
  - Cyclical: Use normalized earnings

### Forward P/E
- Uses analyst estimates for next year's earnings
- Better reflects future expectations
- Compare to trailing P/E for growth trajectory

### PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)
- **Formula**: P/E Ratio / Expected Annual EPS Growth Rate
- **Interpretation**:
  - <1.0 = potentially undervalued
  - ~1.0 = fairly valued
  - >2.0 = potentially overvalued
- Adjusts P/E for growth expectations

### Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio
- **Formula**: Market Cap / Annual Revenue
- **Use**: Good for unprofitable companies or comparing growth companies
- Compare to peers and historical range

### Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio
- **Formula**: Stock Price / Book Value Per Share
- **Use**: Best for asset-heavy businesses (banks, real estate, manufacturing)
- <1.0 may indicate undervaluation or asset quality concerns
- Less relevant for asset-light businesses (software, services)

### Enterprise Value / EBITDA (EV/EBITDA)
- **Formula**: (Market Cap + Debt - Cash) / EBITDA
- **Advantages**: Accounts for capital structure, good for comparing leveraged companies
- **Typical Ranges**:
  - <10x = potentially undervalued
  - 10-15x = fair value range
  - >20x = premium valuation or high growth expectations

### Price-to-Free Cash Flow (P/FCF)
- **Formula**: Market Cap / Annual Free Cash Flow
- Often more reliable than P/E (harder to manipulate cash flow)
- Compare to historical range and peers

## Relative Valuation Approach

### Peer Comparison
1. Identify 3-5 comparable companies (same industry, similar size/model)
2. Compare key ratios: P/E, P/S, EV/EBITDA, P/FCF
3. Calculate peer median/average
4. Determine if target company trades at premium or discount
5. Assess if premium/discount is justified by:
   - Superior growth rates
   - Better margins
   - Stronger competitive position
   - Lower risk profile

### Industry Benchmarks
- Compare to sector averages
- Consider where company falls in industry lifecycle
- Account for competitive position

## Absolute Valuation Considerations

### Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Conceptual Framework
While full DCF modeling is complex, understand the key drivers:

**Components**:
1. **Free Cash Flow Projections**: Estimate 5-10 years of FCF
2. **Growth Rate**: Revenue and margin assumptions
3. **Discount Rate (WACC)**: Cost of capital (typically 8-12%)
4. **Terminal Value**: Value beyond projection period
5. **Present Value**: Discount all cash flows to today

**Key Assumptions to Consider**:
- Revenue growth rate (be conservative)
- Operating margin trajectory
- Capital expenditure requirements
- Working capital needs
- Terminal growth rate (2-3% typical)
- Discount rate (use WACC or add risk premium)

**Sanity Checks**:
- Does implied valuation align with peer multiples?
- Are growth assumptions realistic vs. historical performance?
- Does terminal value dominate total value? (Red flag if >80%)

## Valuation Summary Framework

For comprehensive analysis, create a valuation summary:

1. **Multiple Approaches**:
   - Current P/E vs. historical range
   - Current P/E vs. peers
   - PEG ratio assessment
   - EV/EBITDA vs. peers
   - Price-to-FCF analysis

2. **Fair Value Estimate Range**:
   - Bull case (optimistic assumptions)
   - Base case (realistic assumptions)
   - Bear case (conservative assumptions)

3. **Margin of Safety**:
   - Compare current price to fair value estimate
   - Consider buying if trading 20-30% below fair value
   - Factor in confidence level and risk

## Context Considerations

- **Growth Stage**: High-growth companies justify premium valuations
- **Profitability**: Unprofitable companies require different metrics (P/S, revenue growth)
- **Industry**: Different sectors have different normal valuation ranges
- **Market Conditions**: Bull markets = higher multiples; bear markets = compression
- **Company-Specific**: Quality of earnings, competitive moat, management quality
